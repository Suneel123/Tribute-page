Calyampudi Radhakrishna Rao

The only Indian to receive the prestigious Guy Medal for brilliance in Statistics

National Medal of Science, USA in 2002, from the American president, G.W.Bush, calling him, "a prophet of the new age".

"When I think of modern statistics, Dr C.R.Rao features on the top of the list. He once said that statistics is the technology of finding the invisible and measuring the immeasurable."

Abdul Kalam, Bharat Ratna

      The living legend and doyen of Indian Statistics, Prof. C.R.Rao was awarded the Guy Medal in Gold of the Royal Statistical Society, UK on the 29th of the June, 2011 "For his fundamental contributions to statistical theory and methodology, including unbiased estimation, variance reduction by sufficiency, efficiency of estimation, information geometry, as well as the application of matrix theory in linear statistical inference".

Timeline of C.R.Rao

  • Born on 10 September 1920 in Huvvina Hadagalli, then in the integrated Maras Province, now in the state of Karnataka.
  • His mother A.Lakshmi Kanthamma and his father C.Doraswamy Naidu was an Inspector of Police whose job required the family to move from place to place ever three years.
  • He was admitted to class 2 in 1925 when he was only five years old.
  • Rao completed classes 2 and 3 in Gudur, classes 4 and 5 in Nuzvid and the first and second forms in Nandigama, all in the present state of Andhra Pradesh.
  • After retirement, Rao's father decided to settle down in Visakapatnam, a costal city in Andhra Pradesh.
  • Rao developed research interest in mathematics, when he was a student of the B A (Hons) degree course at the age of 17 in Andhra University.
  • He obtained the B A (Hons) degree in mathematics at the age of 19, with a first class and first rank, in Visakapatnam.
  • He obtained the M A degree at the Andhra University in Waltair, Andhra Pradesh in 1941, with a first class and first rank.
  • After M A, he appeared for job interview in Calcutta and got rejected. During his stay in Calcutta, he met one Subramanian who was emploed in Bombay, but had been sent to Calcutta for training in statistics at Indian Statistical Institute (ISI). This chance encounter led Rao to join the training programme in statistics at ISI hoping that with some additional qualifition he could get some job.
  • Rao joined in ISI in 1941 at the age of 20 and started doing research by himself and pulishing papers. He received M A degree in statistics from Calcutta University in 1943 with a first class, first rank and a highest percentage of marks which remains unbroken upto now.
  • With two Master's degrees, Rao was given the position of a research scholar at ISI in 1943 and a part time job in Calcutta University to teach a course in statistics.
  • A request from the Department of Anthropology, Cambridge University was sent to ISI in 1946 to send a person to analyse measurements made on human skeletons using the method of Mahalanobis D-square statistic. Rao was sent to Cambridge by Mahalanobis and he worked in Cambridge for two years (1946 - 1948) as a visiting scholar at Cambridge University Museun of Archaeology and Anthropology.
  • During this period, Rao also registered for Ph D degree under the supervision of Ronald A. Fisher, a distinguished statistician. Rao received his Ph D in 1948 based on new multivariate methodology, 'Multivariate Analysis of Variance (MANOVA).'
  • In 1948, he returned to ISI and was appointed as Professor at the young age of 28 'in recognition of his creativity'.
  • He worked for 40 years in ISI in various positions.
  • He took mandatory retirement at the age of 60.
  • He wanted a suitable job in India without any administrative responsibilities to continue his research work. As this was not possible, he acccepted positions of distinguished peofessorships offered to him with minimal teaching responsibilities in USA.
  • He worked for another 30 years as University Professor. At the University of pittsburg for 8 years and as Eberly Chair Professor of Statistics at The Pennsylvania State University for 13 years.
  • He retired from Penn State at the age of 81, but continued doing research as Director of the Center for Multivariate Analysis at Penn State until 2008.
  • At the age 93 he has position as a Research Professor at the University of Buffalo.
  • As of today (26th May, 2017) his age is 96.
  • He is author of 14 books and 475 research papers. He has edited to date 37 volumes of Handbooks of Statistics in different areas of application of statistics.
